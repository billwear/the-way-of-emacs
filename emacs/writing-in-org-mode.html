The Dramatic Story Engine: A Fiction Framework in Org-mode

Posted April 12, 2025 by Bill

Every story needs structure. Not a formula. A framework. Something solid enough to support creativity — and flexible enough to evolve with it. That’s what the Dramatic Story Engine is designed to do.

I built it because I was tired of writing notes that never aligned, outlines that buckled under revision, and character arcs that drifted out to sea. What I needed was a living structure. Something modular. Something in Emacs. And something I could tweak without rewriting a whole book.

So I created the Dramatic Story Engine — a small set of flexible narrative components managed in Org-mode, designed to capture the deep structure of a story without turning the writing into homework.

What is the Dramatic Story Engine?

It’s a way to track how your story functions, using a few core ideas:

  • Conflict Frames: High-level tensions that power your plot and your theme.
  • Character Functions: Narrative roles that evolve across the story.
  • Domains: The arenas where story change happens — personal, relational, societal, and unknown.
  • Pressure Tracks: Where the story applies change, and how characters push back or adapt.

You don’t need to fill them all in at once. You grow them as your story grows. And because it’s Org-mode, everything stays linkable, searchable, and version-controlled.

Conflict Frames: Tension is Structure

Every story has a central conflict — but great stories hum with layers of tension. The engine helps you name and track these as Conflict Frames: opposing ideas that power scenes and character decisions.

Example:


* Conflict Frames                                      :structure:
** Truth vs Comfort
- Lira believes knowing the truth will free her.
- Tallis clings to comfort: "We survived this long not asking questions."
- The artifact destabilizes both.
** Control vs Wildness
- The ship’s AI wants predictability.
- Neptune’s magnetic shifts seem... alive.

These aren’t “themes” you declare. They’re living tensions. You can tag scenes with these frames to see how they evolve.

Character Functions: Who Drives What?

This isn’t about archetypes — it’s about function. Ask: What job does each character serve in the story’s dramatic machinery?


* Character Functions                                   :characters:
** Protagonist: Lira Okoye
- Drives pursuit of the anomaly.
- Interrogates the archive, Neptune, herself.
** Catalyst: Tallis
- Forces uncomfortable questions.
- Challenges Lira’s certainty and habits.
** Foil: Ship AI "Sefer"
- Represents control, caution, limitation.

This helps you balance scenes. Is the Protagonist acting? Is the Catalyst disrupting? Is the Foil opposing enough?

Story Domains: Where the Drama Happens

Each story operates on multiple levels — not just events, but implications. I track those with four domains:

  • Inner: personal identity, memory, self-deceit
  • Relational: power dynamics, trust, obligation
  • Structural: institutions, cultures, norms
  • Unknown: alien, divine, chaotic, metaphysical

Scenes or chapters get tagged with these domains to see what kind of story you’re actually telling.


* Domains
** Relational: Lira & Tallis
- Their shared trauma hides deeper mistrust.
** Structural: The scientific protocols
- The mission’s rules prevent discovery.
** Unknown: The magnetic anomaly
- It speaks, but no one's sure how.

Tracking Change: The Pressure Map

Good stories push people. Bad ones let them stay the same. A Pressure Map asks: Where is the story applying pressure, and where are characters resisting or growing?


* Pressure Map                                          :evolution:
** Lira
- Pressure: Must abandon archive neutrality
- Pushback: Clings to her scientist role
- Change: Begins questioning foundational beliefs
** Tallis
- Pressure: Needs to accept loss
- Pushback: Tries to act as protector
- Change: Starts letting Lira take the lead

This lets you revise for emotional weight. Too little pressure = flat story. No pushback = unbelievable change.

How I Use It While Writing

I start with one or two Conflict Frames, sketch rough Character Functions, and keep the Pressure Map open as a checklist during edits. I don’t write linearly — this gives me anchor points to return to, and a way to track how the story is behaving over time.

Every week or so, I revise the engine itself: add a new tension, rewrite a function, remove an arc that’s gone nowhere. This system is alive.

Try It Yourself (Template)

Here’s a dead-simple Org template to get started:


* Conflict Frames
** {{Opposition A vs B}}
- {{Initial expression}}
- {{Scene moments}}

* Character Functions
** {{Character Name}} - {{Function}}
- {{Drives/pushes/blocks}}

* Pressure Map
** {{Character Name}}
- Pressure:
- Pushback:
- Change:

* Domains
** {{Scene/Relationship}} - {{Domain(s)}}

You can even capture new story elements using `org-capture`, or link to these frames from scene notes or TODOs.
