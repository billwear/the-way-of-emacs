How I Prepare for My Day

Some mornings I wake up and feel like a hero. Other mornings I wake up and feel like I’ve been smuggled into the day by mistake. That’s why I stopped winging it and built a morning routine in Org mode that feels more like architecture than aspiration.

I use Emacs to schedule my entire morning down to the minute. Not because I want to be a robot, but because I need something stable to stand on before the day starts slipping sideways. Here's what my morning agenda looks like:

That might look like overkill, but it’s the opposite. It’s relief. I don’t have to think about what comes next—I just read it and do it. Each line is a kindness to my future self.

Why the Schedule is Tightly Timed

Each task is SCHEDULED with an :Effort: property in Org mode, so I can visualize how the morning stacks up. That includes tiny tasks—like taking magnesium or shaving—because those are the ones I’m most likely to skip if they’re not written down.

Clustering by Context

Most of my hygiene tasks happen in the bathroom. That’s not just convenient—it’s intentional. ADHD brains lose energy jumping contexts, so I group tasks by location. The result is less friction, fewer forgotten steps, and more flow.

The Emologent Check-In

Before hygiene, I check in emotionally. It’s a structured journal format I call my “emologent,” and it helps me scan my emotional weather, name distractions, and ground myself. It’s not therapy, but it keeps me from jumping straight into the chaos of the world.

Why I Think This Works

  • It eliminates decision fatigue. I don’t wonder what’s next. I just do the next thing.
  • It gives me small wins early. Checking off "take Mg" is silly, but it starts a streak.
  • It matches how my brain works. Structured but flexible. Ordered but personal.
  • It’s a ritual, not a rule. I don’t follow it perfectly. But it carries me when I can’t carry myself.
  • It externalizes memory. I don’t rely on recall—I rely on rhythm.

This isn’t about squeezing more out of my morning. It’s about not losing it to friction and fuzziness. The day will throw enough at me. This gives me a shape to stand in first.
