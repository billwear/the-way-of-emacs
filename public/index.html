The Way of Emacs

March 4, 2025

Attention dictates our lives; labels don't. How we sort signal from noise defines our mind-share, and in turn, our reality. Chasing dopamine drains our time, energy, and focus, chipping away our income, relationships, and well-being. Thinking deeply, carefully, and honestly about our lives builds character, resilience, and capability.

It's always been this way. Smartphones and social media didn't create this problem, but they are hoping to monetize it in some new and unique ways. Being on carefully-curated display 24/7 makes the problem larger, and creates more thrash -- but the social problem of appearance versus reality seems to be universal and eternal.

As a dedicated user of the text editor Emacs -- often called "a great operating [computer] system that desperately needs a text editor" -- I've found a lot more than just a tool set. It's helped me firm up a philosophy that counters the dopamine addiction and brings me face-to-face with myself.

What I'm sharing here isn’t that much about learning Emacs; it's mostly about learning useful lessons from the principles that brought Emacs about. There's a unique system for cutting through static and shaping life on your own terms. Emacs embodies this philosophy, but you don’t need to use it to benefit from these insights. Here, I'm treating Emacs as a transferable framework for a composable, modular, and intentional life.

It's worthwhile to discover what’s possible when you stop accepting defaults and start designing your own way forward:

  • Life works well as a modular system. Everything can be a building block. Skills, habits, workflows, mistakes, and even horrible experiences can be stepping stones to independence and personal understanding.
  • The pursuit of meaningful signal matters. Signal isn't the next meme, fad, or fashion choice. Those are fun, but they're not the point, and they don't create a lot of meaning or lasting value.
  • Thoughtful control, based on uncompromising honesty, wins most of the time. Mastery isn’t about following rules; it’s about understanding how to shape your own system.
  • Customization matters a lot more than convention. The best solutions are the ones that fit you. Build, tweak, and refine as you will, but never blindly settle for popular defaults.
  • Straightforward thinking rarely leads you astray. The world can be a labyrinth of forced choices and "me, too" moments, but that's not how you build a lasting future or create a meaningful legacy.

The Way of Emacs means taking control of your thinking and your decisions; living deliberately within the boundaries you encounter; dropping what doesn't work for you; and hacking the rest so it becomes a life well-lived.
