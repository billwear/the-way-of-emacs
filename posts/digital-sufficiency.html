Digital Sufficiency: The Right Amount of Technology

March 11, 2025

Technology should serve us, not the other way around. But in an era where smartphones are engineered for endless distraction, it’s easy to slip into passive consumption. The question isn’t **how little** tech we can use—it’s how to use just the right amount.

I run Emacs on my phone. My Android launcher? It’s a command line interface (Yantra Launcher). No icons, no swipe-to-scroll dopamine loops. If I want to open an app, I type its name. This setup forces **intentionality**—if I’m picking up my phone, it’s because I need to do something, not because I’m aimlessly scrolling.

Minimalism vs. Sufficiency

People talk a lot about **digital minimalism**, the idea of stripping away everything unnecessary. But minimalism can be extreme—reducing tech to the point where it **hinders productivity instead of helping**. Sufficiency is different. It’s about having exactly what you need, no more, no less. Just enough tools to be effective, without the excess noise.

Deliberate Use vs. Default Use

Most people use technology the way it’s handed to them—default settings, preloaded apps, infinite notifications. But those defaults weren’t designed for your benefit; they were designed to keep you **engaged, addicted, and profitable** to someone else.

Running Emacs on my phone isn’t about being hardcore. It’s about shaping my environment to match how I want to think and work. A launcher that makes me type means I have to **know what I’m doing before I do it**. That one-second barrier is enough to make mindless browsing feel like effort—and that’s the point.

Finding Your Own Digital Sufficiency

You don’t have to switch to a command-line launcher or run Emacs on your phone to apply this thinking. But take a moment to ask: **Is my tech serving me, or am I serving it?**

Some ways to reclaim control:

  • Turn off non-essential notifications.
  • Remove apps that don’t provide real value.
  • Use tools that require **deliberate action** instead of passive scrolling.
  • Set up your environment to encourage **focus and intentional use**.

Digital sufficiency isn’t about rejecting technology—it’s about using **just the right amount** to stay effective and in control. What would that look like for you?
